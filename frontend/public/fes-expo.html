The expo is where work is encountered: displayed and engaged with at the viewer's own pace. The showcase is a structured programme in which work is experienced sequentially, curated, timed, and fully immersive. The programme will indicate which format applies to each work.

Memory Engines, Imagination Engines, and Future Engines. Each is a distinct curatorial strand; together they form the architecture for the 2026 programme.
The festival draws digital creatives, coders, animators, sound artists, archivists, urban planners, climate technologists, investors, educators, policymakers, and students. The primary audience is 18 to 35; the legacy audience extends to 70. Both are welcome.
A community-managed knowledge system is a digital archive or database built and governed by the community whose knowledge it holds, rather than by an external institution. These platforms allow communities to determine what is preserved, how it is classified, who can access it, and under what conditions. A direct response to the misattribution and displacement of African knowledge.
AI tools, particularly large language models and classification systems, are increasingly used to process, organise, and surface archival material. When those tools are built on African knowledge systems and governed by African practitioners, they become instruments of sovereignty. When they are not, they risk reproducing the same misattributions that they were meant to correct.
No. AI is the dominant technology of the moment and receives significant attention. But Imagination Engines addresses the full range of tools through which African creatives are expanding what is possible: generative media, synthetic sound, algorithmic curation, immersive environments, and the policy and ethical frameworks that govern all of it.
